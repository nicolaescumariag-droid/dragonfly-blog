The Dragonfly: Ancient Hunter of the Air

Long before dinosaurs walked the Earth, dragonflies were already in the sky. Fossils show their ancestors flying over 300 million years ago, some with wingspans as wide as a hawk's. Today's dragonflies are smaller, but they remain one of nature's most skilled and fascinating hunters.

A Life That Begins in Water

Most people only notice dragonflies darting over ponds in summer, but they actually spend the majority of their lives underwater. After hatching, a dragonfly lives as a nymph beneath the surface, sometimes for several years. During this stage it is a fierce predator in its own right, feeding on tadpoles, small fish, and other insects. Only when it is ready does it climb out of the water, shed its skin, and emerge with wings.

Masters of Flight

Few creatures can match a dragonfly in the air. They can fly forwards, backwards, and even hover in place. Their two pairs of wings move independently, giving them remarkable control. This agility makes them deadly hunters — studies suggest they catch their prey on more than 90 percent of their attempts, a success rate almost unheard of in the animal world.

Eyes Like No Other

A dragonfly's head is almost entirely eyes. Each eye contains thousands of tiny lenses, giving the insect nearly 360-degree vision. This is how they spot the smallest movement of a mosquito or fly from a distance, then intercept it mid-air with precision.

A Small Wonder Worth Noticing

The next time you see a dragonfly resting on a reed or hovering over a pond, take a moment to watch. You are looking at a survivor — an animal whose design has worked for hundreds of millions of years, and still works beautifully today.

Fun Facts About Dragonflies

  • Dragonflies have been around for more than 300 million years, predating the dinosaurs.
  • They can fly in all directions — forwards, backwards, sideways, and straight up.
  • Some species can reach speeds of up to 35 miles per hour.
  1. They are found on every continent except Antarctica.
  2. Dragonflies catch their prey mid-flight rather than on the ground.
  3. A dragonfly's eyes contain up to 30,000 individual lenses.
